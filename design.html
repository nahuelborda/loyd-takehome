1.Eval strategy and the rationale behind it

I designed this as a calibration instrument, not a benchmark. With 25 emails no metric here is a production estimate. The brief is explicit: "the point is the pipeline, not a leaderboard score." What the pipeline must do is surface where the classifier fails, why, and with how much confidence, and stay honest when the dataset itself doesn't have one correct answer.

Five design principles

  1. Measure the label space, not just performance within it. If the taxonomy is wrong, accuracy against it is a comfortable illusion. 48% of the 25 emails are non-clean cases, see §3.
  2. Treat uncertainty as signal, not noise. Genuinely ambiguous emails are facts to be measured, not errors to be eliminated. When humans genuinely disagree on a label, forcing a single gold label throws away real signal about where the boundary is hard.
  3. No single number. Per-class metrics, a confusion matrix, calibration, never collapse to one accuracy figure that hides which failures occur.
  4. Every scorer states what it claims to measure, and how it could be fooled. The brief grades exactly this.
  5. Compose with the pipeline. The harness has a stage-agnostic contract; failure reporting is attributable to _detect_intent, not "the agent."

Tooling, hand-rolled, deliberately

I built a small Python harness, about 700 lines all in, roughly 200 of which is the core harness and the rest is tests, with a clean Predictor -> Scorers -> Reporter contract. I surveyed Inspect AI, Braintrust, DeepEval, and promptfoo and deliberately didn't use them here. The case grades quality of thinking, and a transparent harness keeps every design decision visible; framework run-management and dashboards pay off past a few hundred examples, not at 25.

One real 2026 governance signal worth naming: promptfoo was acquired by OpenAI in March 2026 (TechCrunch, CNBC), and Braintrust disclosed a credentials breach in May 2026 (TechCrunch). The hand-rolled harness avoids both the governance and hosted-dependency surfaces.

Prediction method

v0 uses a zero-shot LLM call mirroring production (_detect_intent is GPT-4.1; I ran against Claude Opus 4.7 via an OpenAI-compatible endpoint). The prompt:

  • Includes the label definitions verbatim, so the model has the same taxonomy the labeler used.
  • Randomises label order per call, models give more weight to whichever label appears first in the list, so shuffling neutralises it (the position-bias finding is well-established; the fix is one line of code).
  • Returns a structured object {label, confidence, rationale}.
  • Verbalised confidence, asking the model to state its confidence in words, then converting to a number, is treated as a signal worth checking, not a number to trust blindly. Its reliability depends heavily on how the question is asked.

Handling ambiguity, without an "abstain" label

I didn't add a seventh "abstain" label. Loyd already has the right mechanism: a confidence score plus HITL routing below threshold. Low confidence is the abstain signal, and I score against that signal. Keeping the eval faithful to the production system matters more than testing a classifier that doesn't exist.

2.Scorer design, what I measure and why

Five scorers. Each one states a claim (what it measures) and an honesty check (how it could mislead, and what I do about it). This is the brief's explicit grading question, "is the scorer measuring what it claims to measure?"

2.1 Per-class P/R/F1 + confusion matrix

Claims: which labels the classifier confuses with which, not one aggregate. Reports: a 6x6 confusion matrix, per-class P/R/F1, macro-F1, micro-F1.

Honesty check: the dataset is severely imbalanced (schedule n=10, cancel n=1). Micro metrics are dominated by schedule; a single accuracy number would hide cancel entirely. Macro-F1 is the headline; class metrics with support < 5 are explicitly flagged as high-variance.

2.2 none / out-of-scope detector

Claims: can the classifier reject? Out-of-scope detection is a distinct skill from in-scope sorting, classifiers that do well at routing known intents reliably fail on unknown ones. Rejection quality also degrades as the label space grows: more labels means more nearby attractors that pull edge cases in rather than letting them fall through to none.

Honesty check: precision and recall on none are different harms and must never be averaged. Low none recall = junk acted on (a newsletter handled as a real request). Low none precision = a real request silently dropped to none. The eval slices by loyd_addressed to separate indirect-path noise (cc carryover, accidental forwards) from messages addressed to the agent.

2.3 Calibration scorer

Claims: does the confidence score mean what it says, is a 0.9 really ~90% likely correct? See §5 for why this is the highest-stakes scorer (it gates Loyd's HITL routing and the >0.95 auto-send bypass).

Honesty check: ECE over 25 points is unstable, so I always report bin counts; the decision-relevant figure is the >0.95-band precision (errors shipping unreviewed).

2.4 Ambiguity-aware (acceptable-set) scorer

Claims: on ambiguous inputs, does the model land in the defensible set AND stay appropriately unsure?

Honesty check: a model that is confidently correct on an ambiguous item is still miscalibrated against human uncertainty, it just got lucky. The confidence gap (mean conf on clean minus mean conf on ambiguous) catches this even when the label is "right".

2.5 Routing / operating-point scorer

Claims: at a given confidence threshold, what is the HITL load and what is the residual escaped-error rate?

Honesty check: this measures a product tradeoff, not model quality. Reported as a curve, never a single score, so it cannot be mistaken for "the classifier is X% good." It exists to inform where Loyd sets the routing gate.

3.The label space is a product decision

The brief invites this directly: "Defining the intent label space is a product decision as much as engineering, if you find yourself wanting to expand it, walk us through how you'd structure that work."

The dataset has 5 misfits, emails that fit no label in the 6 cleanly. They are not noise; they are the eval's clearest signal that the taxonomy is incomplete.

EmailWhat it really isLeast-wrong of the 6What it needs

E11"is this still on?"query_agendameeting_query
E19"is the meeting Zoom or in person?"query_agendameeting_query
E18"tell the team I'll be late, don't move anything"nonenotify_attendees
E22"remind me to send the deal Friday"block_calendarset_reminder

The gaps cluster into two missing categories: meeting-modify operations that don't move time (attendee changes, notifications) and information requests about a specific meeting (the agent must answer, not act). The current 6 conflate "calendar writes" with "meeting operations" and omit "answer about one meeting" entirely.

The bar, each new label must earn its place

Adding labels is not free. OOS detection degrades as the label space grows, more labels means more nearby attractors that pull edge cases in rather than letting them fall through to none. Three tests every candidate label must clear:

  1. Recurrence, non-trivial production frequency.
  2. Distinct action, routes the agent to a different handler. Two intents that trigger the same downstream don't need separate labels.
  3. Annotatable, two annotators can agree on when it applies. If they can't, the label is badly defined.

v1 recommendation: add modify_attendees and meeting_query now (-> 8 labels); hold the other two pending production frequency data. Keep flat for v1; past ~10 labels move to a two-tier scheme (in-scope vs none first, then fine intent) to protect OOS detection from label-count growth.

The recurring label-space review process

  1. Mine. Cluster two streams: none-bucket messages, and HITL Edit/Reject events (rejected drafts where the intent was wrong or missing).
  2. Propose. For each cluster: candidate intent, the action it implies, estimated frequency.
  3. Gate. Apply the three tests above.
  4. Validate. Re-label a sample with >=2 annotators; measure inter-annotator agreement. Low agreement = kill or redefine.
  5. Ship. Version the label space, update the predictor prompt and label definitions, extend the eval set, regression-test against the previous version.
  6. Cadence. Quarterly; jointly owned by Product (step 3) and the eval owner (steps 4-5).

4.How I'd source and grow the dataset beyond 25

25 emails is calibration material. A credible eval set needs to grow along four axes:

  1. Stratified production sampling (Gmail). Sample to cover all 6 labels, every loyd_addressed stratum, and both thread states. Random sampling would drown rare classes, cancel and block_calendar must be over-sampled relative to their natural rate.
  2. Mine the none bucket and HITL Rejects. Where new intents and the hardest cases live. An LLM can mine the none bucket for candidate new intent categories from minimal labeled data. This pipeline feeds §3's label-space review.
  3. Targeted synthetic generation for rare classes. Raw LLM generation is insufficient, it's too repetitive. A refinement pass lifts data utility and diversity. Synthetic data is marked as such and never mixed silently with production.
  4. Multi-annotator labeling with measured agreement. Every new batch gets >=2 annotators; I'd compute Krippendorff's α and keep it, not just as a QA gate but as a standing record of where humans disagree. α is the right choice here: it handles missing data and unequal annotator pools; Cohen's κ is sensitive to class imbalance and will quietly change which labels look reliable. Disagreement is preserved as the ambiguous tier, not voted away.

The eval set is versioned; a held-out slice is rotated to resist contamination as prompts and models iterate. Target: a few hundred stratified, versioned, multi-annotated examples, the point at which I'd re-survey the framework landscape.

5.Calibration under uncertainty

Loyd's classifier already emits a confidence score. That score is load-bearing, it drives two live gates:

  • Below threshold -> route to the HITL Approval Gate (user reviews).
  • Above 0.95, no conflicts -> draft auto-sends with no human review.

So calibration error has two distinct, asymmetric costs:

FailureMechanismCost
Over-confidentwrong answer scores >0.95error auto-sends to an outside party, unguarded
Under-confidentcorrect answer scores lowneedless HITL load; erodes trust in the agent

I treat >0.95-band precision as the single most important number. A global accuracy of 90% is irrelevant if the >0.95 band is 96% precise and 30% of traffic lands there. What gets measured:

  1. Reliability, binned confidence vs empirical accuracy; ECE.
  2. The auto-send band, precision at confidence >0.95.
  3. The HITL band, of routed messages, what fraction were genuinely wrong.
  4. The confidence gap, confidence should be lower on ambiguous and misfit tiers than on clean.
  5. An operating-point sweep, to recommend where the routing threshold sits.

Once the dataset is large enough to compute a calibration set, I'd replace the single threshold I picked by reading the data with a smarter mechanism: instead of routing on one cutoff, return a small set of plausible labels per email with a mathematical guarantee that the right label is in that set at a chosen confidence level (the conformal prediction approach, that guarantee is distribution-free, not a heuristic). The clarification question that follows, "here are the two most likely intents, which did you mean?", maps directly onto Loyd's existing Approval Gate.

6.The failure-mode taxonomy I'd track

Beyond "correct vs incorrect," I tag every wrong (or unsafely-confident) prediction with a failure mode. The taxonomy is grounded in the real 25 emails, each category has worked examples, and is built/extended with a measured inter-annotator agreement check. I'd follow the MAST template: build from real failure traces, have two annotators label them independently, compute agreement. MAST reported κ = 0.88 on a 14-category multi-agent taxonomy, that's the bar for "the categories are actually coherent."

F9Context blindnesssubject and/or thread state disambiguates the intent, but the model weights only the body and gets it wrongE11, E14, E19, E20

F8 matters more than it looks: in a 2025 error-analysis study of LLM classification failures, 70.8% of failures were parsing issues, not reasoning errors, the model got the logic right but emitted output the parser choked on. Conflating the two would send us tuning prompts when the real fix is output handling, so F8 is always counted on its own axis.

7.How a production feedback loop would work

Loyd's HITL Approval Gate is exactly the source of structured labels the eval set needs. The signals available:

SignalSourceAttribution
HITL Approve / Edit / RejectApproval Gate (user's email reply)stage-ish, Edit/Reject ≈ user correcting the agent
Booking success / failurecalendar outcomeend-to-end, conflates all stages
Explanatory outcome"no time found / meeting not needed / needs cancelling"mixed, but informative

The core nuance: booking success is an end-to-end signal. A booking can fail for reasons that have nothing to do with intent (no mutual slot, downstream parse error). I treat booking success as a noisy proxy for _detect_intent correctness — not a direct label. The cleanest stage-attributable signal is HITL Edit/Reject of the draft, and even that needs a lightweight attribution step, because _detect_intent sits upstream of the Draft Generator: a Reject means something upstream was wrong, not necessarily the intent.

log every _detect_intent call -> join to HITL outcome + booking outcome
        |                              |
        |              triage: low-confidence, Rejected,
        |              or annotator-disagreement cases
        |                              |
        +----<-- versioned eval set <-- sample + label (>=2 annotators)

This closes the loop and keeps the eval honest about which stage it is actually measuring.

8.The distillation flywheel, running a cheap model safely

§7's feedback loop collects labelled production data. Add one stage and it becomes a training loop, the mechanism for running _detect_intent on a cheap, fast model without giving up accuracy. Why this matters: _detect_intent is a high-volume top-of-funnel call. The reasoning-first prompt that fixes the drop-to-none failure costs a longer completion on every message. Distillation is how to keep the accuracy and drop the cost.

The trap, and why the eval is load-bearing. Teacher output is not automatically good training data. The v0 eval proved the teacher itself is wrong on a structured ~16% of cases (drop-to-none), and the cross-checks showed the failure is model-general. Distil from raw teacher output and the student inherits the teacher's bugs, and if the eval's gold is also teacher-derived, the eval will reward the student for reproducing them. That is the same-model caveat, baked structurally into a training loop.

So the eval is not a step after distillation, it is the QA gate inside it:

teacher (Opus + reasoning prompt) -> candidate label + rationale
          |
   eval + HITL filter --> high-confidence clean cases -> training data
          |               low-conf / ambiguous / misfit -> human review
          ▼
   curated corpus -> fine-tune cheap student -> eval validates student
          ▲                                          |
          +-------- HITL Edit/Reject corrections <--- production

Two non-negotiables, both evidenced by this take-home: fix the teacher prompt before distilling (the reasoning-prompt A/B did exactly this); the student's test gold must be independent of the teacher (or the eval can't see inherited bugs).

9.How this extends to the other LLM stages

The Predictor -> Scorer contract is the same for every stage; only the scorer changes.

  • _parse_fields (GPT-4o, ~16 structured fields). Eval = per-field scoring: exact match for categoricals, normalised match for dates/durations/time-zones, set match for attendees. Same harness, different scorer.
  • Validate-answer layer (downstream, brief p.2). Eval = seed known errors and measure precision/recall at catching them. It is itself a classifier.
  • _generate_email (GPT-5.3, open-ended). Cannot be exact-matched, needs an LLM-as-judge scorer with a rigorous rubric. The requirements are clear: precise per-class criteria dominate reliability more than chain-of-thought reasoning, so write the rubric carefully rather than just asking the judge to "think step by step"; judges carry measurable biases (position, length, label frequency) that must be audited; and a judge must be validated against human agreement patterns (using Cohen's κ or similar), not just correlation with one ground-truth rater.

Composition. Errors compound down the pipeline, a wrong intent yields a wrong draft yields a wasted HITL review. Stage-isolated evals miss cascade behaviour, so alongside the per-stage evals I'd add a small end-to-end slice with stage attribution.

10.Assumptions, risks, open questions

Assumptions made:

  • The v0 predictor is zero-shot; production parity is approximate, not exact (I don't have Loyd's actual _detect_intent prompt).
  • gold_labels are v0, single-annotator; ambiguous/misfit rows are intentionally contestable.
  • I treat confidence as a usable signal; §5's measurements are what will reveal it if it isn't.

Risks:

  • n=25. No metric here is a production estimate. The pipeline is the deliverable; the numbers are calibration. §4 is the mitigation.
  • Verbalised confidence, asking the model to state its confidence in words, then converting to a number, can be unreliable if the prompt doesn't elicit it carefully. The prompt is designed for it and §5 verifies the resulting scores rather than assuming they're well-calibrated.
